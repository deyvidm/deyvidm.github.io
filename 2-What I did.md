# Business in the front 

I briefly did some front-end work in the beginning and end of my co-op. I very rarely enjoy writing Javascript and HTML, and this pattern continued. At the end of the day, I was paid to do my job, and my job was to churn out some buttons and tables. 

As unpleasant as they were, these tasks helped me gain a better understanding of how a website works. Despite this, I must admit -- every experience makes me a more well-rounded developer.

# Party in the back 

Most of my work was command-line based. A lot of the tools I developed and maintained were headless scripts and scheduled tasks. 

I really enjoyed this part of my job. It was much easier to test all of my programs because I could just write other programs to systematically test every step. My cover letters always include a line about how much I enjoy automating tasks and writing scripts, so it was really satisfying to have it be the main focus of my job for some time. The ease of automated testing helped prevent bugs and errors stemming from laziness regarding testing, which was a fairly common issue when working on front-end tasks.

Later in my co-op, I was put in charge of developing a tool which would process and generate fairly large sets of data. It required me to write distributed systems, which I had never done before. It was very challenging and I did an objectively bad job the first time. When given the chance to try again, I found I was able to do a much better job, making my following attempts significantly more well-designed and successful. 

The main challenges I faced revolved around ensuring that a request was delivered once, and only once. I found that an effective solution was to use pre-existing tools (AWS SQS) instead of rolling my own. 

I was also given an opportunity to design parallel systems. I had better success with this because I could study pre-existing code. Figuring out efficient ways to store logs for every thread was difficult, considering threads and instances would be run hundreds of times a day. 

Due to the large variety of assigned work, I was able to gain plentiful experience across vital computing themes, which is really the most I can hope to take away from a co-op. 

# Reflecting on pre-requisite knowledge 

I wish I could find the words to express how useless my university courses have been regarding anything I've done in my co-ops. I'm truly saddened by how poorly I can communicate the frustration that stems from having to pay $5,000 of hard-earned money every semester to sit in a dusty, dingy room listening to a dull, uninterested professor drone on about stuff neither of us cares about. I'm not sure if I prefer that, or being taught crucial core concepts of software engineering by someone who has literally never worked a day in the software industry -- true fact, I wish I was making it up.

However, my disgust with the department is a separate issue, as is the bloated administration that tirelessly works to provide top of the line disappointment to every student they can reach. 

I truly believe that all the technical experience the program has offered can be distributed between 3 courses -- barely half a semester. I understand that university programs are not designed for "practical knowledge", but even with that in mind, it is nothing short of shameful to have 5 Software Design courses that iterate the same four concepts using the same powerpoint slides but with different background pictures.

Shoutout to Laura, Greg, and Judi for bringing out the best the university could provide. They've been nothing short of awesome every step of the way.
