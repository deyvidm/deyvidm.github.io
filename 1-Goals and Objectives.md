# Learning to speak effectively 

I've always had trouble expressing myself in a technical environment. 
I always aim to preserve meaning without completely sacrificing accuracy, but learning to juggle the terms appropriately has always been a problem. 

I don't think I'm alone in this. I've observed fellow co-workers struggle with the same issues, and I think learning to overcome those difficulties is an integral part of becoming a well-rounded professional -- regardless of career choice. 

I've put a great deal of effort into figuring out how to effectively convey a message, and I think it's served me well. I can confidently say I've improved since my first co-op.

<br/>

# Solve problems more effectively 

This is kind of a dumb goal because it goes without saying. Every developer everywhere always aims to improve their problem-solving skills. It shouldn't need to be explicitly stated, but here we are nonetheless. 

I was able to improve simply by being exposed to new problems and tools. I can't think of anything that rivals experience when discussing improvement. In theory, I could read every PHP tutorial ever written, but still not improve as much as I would from maintaining and creating solutions. 

I've learned a lot about web technologies. PHP and AWS were my bread and butter, sprinkled with the salty likes of Javascript and HTML. I feel significantly more experienced in these areas than when I started, which is always a good feeling to walk away with.

<br/>

# Team building and relations 

I wasn't happy with how my first co-op ended. I wasn't really part of a team, and I didn't learn much beyond effective time-wasting methods. 

I noticed a vast difference when I entered AdKnown. I'm not sure if it's because they are used to employing a lot of co-ops, or if there are just a lot of good-natured guys <sup>[1]</sup>, but there was a very obvious difference in the surrounding atmosphere. The culture and attitude encouraged working together and collaborating. There was a weekly team lunch, paid for by the company, which really contributed to a sense camaraderie amongst team members.

There was a large following, cult-like in many ways, surrounding the ranked billiards ladder. I took it upon myself to make friends by crushing anyone who dared face me on the faded felt stadium. Losing 10 games in a row served as a stinging reminder that I don't actually know how to play pool. 

I had cemented my position at last place. None dared take the title from me. They kinda couldn't either. My name was written in marker on the whiteboard for so long that we needed special solvents to remove it when I finally made the climb to second-last. 

Four months of gruelling summer training (_I lost the training montage during the move_) turned me billiard-ball-sinking-machine, provided the cue ball was perfectly lined up, and the object ball was fairly close to the pocket. 

Nothing could stand in my way, except the entirety of the pool ladder. 
Eventually, when all the top players went on vacation or were too busy to play, I slimed my way into the top 5. It was the proudest moment of my entire career. 

With a few lucky scratches on my opponents' turns, I miraculously snaked my way into the golden 3, which I managed to cling to for a whole 23 minutes.

At the end of the day, I had a great time sharing stories and laughs over a game of pool. It brought me closer to my co-workers and allowed us to speak about our lives outside of work. 

Tim, I know you'll read this -- I'm coming back for your hide.
